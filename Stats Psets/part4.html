Topic 4: Test Statistics

In the previous section, we stated null and alternative hypotheses in terms of the population parameter \(\mu\), but we never observe \(\mu\). Instead, we use sample data to construct estimates of the unknown parameter \(\mu\).

Since we never observe \(\mu\), it's not obvious how we should decide whether the sample data contains enough evidence in favor of \(H_1\) for us to reject \(H_0\). Test statistics provide us with a systematic way of making this decision.

The most common test statistic is the t-statistic. The formula for the t-statistic for a sample average is given by $$t = \frac{\overline{X} - \mu}{s/\sqrt{N}} $$ where \(\overline{X}\) is the sample average, \(s\) is the sample standard deviation, \(N\) is the sample size, and \(\mu\) is the population average under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true.

You should be familiar with the t-statistic from course lectures. Because of the Central Limit Theorem, we know that the t-statistic is approximately normally distributed when \(N>30\). This crucial result allows us to use sample data generated from any unknown distribution to form a test statistic with a known distribution (standard normal). All we need are 3 basic sample statistics: the sample average, the sample standard deviation, and the sample size!

Because we know the distribution of a standard normal random variable, we are able to make statements about the probability of our sample estimate occurring if the null hypothesis is true. What exactly does that mean? We can use an example to clear things up.

Example 3

- Your friend choosing between investment banking (firm A) and software engineering (firm B) goes on Glassdoor and observes salary data from a subset of employees at firm A and firm B. Suppose that starting salaries at firm A are approximately normally distributed with mean \(\mu = 130,000\) and standard deviation \(\sigma = 30,000\) and starting salaries at firm B are approximately normally distributed with mean \(\mu = 120,000\) and standard deviation \(\sigma = 20,000\). These will be our data generating processes.

Using R, construct a random sample of 100 salaries from each of the two firms. Construct the t-statistic for the difference in sample means (this is a 2 sample hypothesis test - you should modify the one-sample t-statistic formula to reflect this). Now repeat this process 500 times and plot the distribution of t-statistics across all the iterations. Is the distribution approximately standard normal?

Note: For reproducibility, you should set a value for the random seed before beginning. If you are confused, please use ChatGPT to help.

Hopefully you are satisfied that the t-statistic has a distribution that is approximately standard normal. In the next section, we will use this fact to construct p-values.
