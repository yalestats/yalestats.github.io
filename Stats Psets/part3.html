Question 1: Stating Hypotheses

We often want to know the relationship between two population parameters. Does an investment banking analyst make more on average than a software engineer? Do new associates at top law firms bill 2000 hours annually?

Even if we collect data on the salaries of every investment banking analyst and software engineer or the billable hours of every legal associate, we still do not observe the population parameters that we want to know about. Why is this? Because every individualâ€™s salary or recorded billable hours is a random variable that is drawn from an underlying data generating process (more on this in the previous conceptual pages). Ultimately, when we ask about a population parameter we are asking about a feature of this underlying model.

The goal of any hypothesis test is to make an inference about these unknown population parameter(s) using sample data. Stating the right hypotheses can be tricky - we will walk through two examples below.

A final word on constructing null and alternative hypotheses: two helpful hints are (1) the null hypothesis always contains an equality (either \(=\), \(\le\), or \(\ge\)) and (2) the alternative hypothesis is the claim for which we are seeking evidence (it would be bad science to fail to reject this claim just because the data doesn't convincingly suggest the opposite relationship holds).
