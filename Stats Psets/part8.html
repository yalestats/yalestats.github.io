Question 2: Evaluating Regression Output

Congratulations! You have been hired as the Chief Data Sorcerer at "FantasyTech," a tech company that brings magical innovations to life.

You are told of a dataset that contained historical records of "FantasyTech" sales over the past 10 years. Each of the 40 rows in the dataset represents the sales data for a particular quarter. You know the variables are given by

\(Y\) - the total number of products sold by "FantasyTech" in the given quarter

\(X\) - the USD amount (in thousands) spent on marketing campaigns to promote "FantasyTech" products during the quarter

Your goal is to recreate a regression model that predicts the future sales growth for "FantasyTech" based on the company's marketing spend. Unfortunately, your predecessor seems to have misplaced this historical sales dataset and only has the following R output - which is missing some information - to show you:

(1) To start, what is the missing value of the standard error on the coefficient for \(X\)?

You should have gotten \(se(\hat{\beta}_1) = 2.703\). This is because $$\frac{\hat{\beta}_1}{se(\hat{\beta}_1)} = t \iff se(\hat{\beta}_1) = \frac{100.907}{37.326} $$

(2) You learn that the "FantasyTech" team has one other useful piece of information about the old regression model. When marketing spending was $400,000, the predicted number of products sold was 40,016.51. Using this information, what is the missing value of \(\hat{\beta}_0\), the intercept coefficient?

The predicted number of products sold \(\hat{Y}\) is given by $$\hat{Y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 X $$ You are told that \(X = 400\) (be careful about the units!), so it must be that $$\hat{\beta}_0 = 40,016.51 - 100.907 \times 400 = -346.29$$

(3) Finally, what is the missing t-statistic for the intercept \(\beta_0\)?

You should have gotten \(t = -0.244\). This is because $$\frac{\hat{\beta}_0}{se(\hat{\beta}_0)} = t \iff t = \frac{-346.29}{1417.973} $$
