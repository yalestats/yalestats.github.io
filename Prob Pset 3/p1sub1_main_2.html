Like in the case when we added 2, the fact that the expected value of Y is 5 times the expected value of X is not a coincidence. This will hold for any number! Unlike when we added, however, note that the variance (and thus standard deviation) have changed. The variance has been multiplied by 25 (5 squared, not a coincidence) and the standard deviation has been multiplied by 5. Like in the case of addition, these are general rules that would have applied no matter what number we multiplied by.

Why does multiplication change the variance while addition does not? Think about what is happening to the “spread” of numbers when we multiply. Before we multiplied, the most extreme values of 1 and 4 were reasonably close. After we multiply these become 5 and 20 respectively, which are much further apart. Thus the variance increases. This is sometimes why the a multiplication factor of a distribution is called the “spread” parameter.
