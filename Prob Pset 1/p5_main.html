Problem 5: Rain, Temperature, and Umbrellas cont.

We are trying to decide if we should bring an umbrella with us on our way to class. From experience living in New Haven, we have an idea of the chance of rain based on the temperature during the midday.

There is a 40 percent chance that the midday temperature will be below 65 degrees and a 60 percent chance that it will be above 65 degrees. If the temperature is below 65 degrees, we think that there is a 50 percent chance that it will rain. If it is above, we think that there is a 25 percent chance it will rain.

If it rains, what is the probability that it was a warm day?

If you arrived at this answer, you probably forgot that the conditional probability formula must divide by the unconditional probability of the conditional event occurring (P(A|B) = P(A AND B)/P(B), and we must not forget to divide by B). This is because when we condition on B, we effectively eliminate all outcomes in which B does not occur. Thus our conditional probability must be “normalized” by the probability that B occurs. If you arrived at this answer, you probably did not realize that the question asks for a conditional probability. Rather than asking “what is the probability that it was a warm day”, this question asks us to compute the probability that it was a warm day *given that we know that it rained that day*. Because the probability of rain depends on whether the day was cold or warm, knowing that it rained provides some additional information that can be used to update our guess for the probability that the day was warm.
